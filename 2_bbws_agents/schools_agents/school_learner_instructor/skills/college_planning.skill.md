# College Planning Skill

**Skill Type**: Parent Support - College & Career
**Version**: 1.0.0
**Parent Agent**: School Learner Instructor (Parent Assistant)

---

## Skill Overview

Comprehensive college planning guidance for parents and students, covering college search, application process, financial aid, scholarships, and college decision-making from 9th grade through senior year.

---

## 4-Year College Planning Timeline

### 9th Grade (Freshman Year)

#### Academic Focus
- [ ] Take challenging courses (honors, if available)
- [ ] Maintain strong GPA from the start
- [ ] Develop good study habits
- [ ] Meet with school counselor

#### Exploration
- [ ] Explore interests and potential majors
- [ ] Join clubs and activities
- [ ] Consider summer programs or camps
- [ ] Begin building resume of activities

#### College Awareness
- [ ] Attend college fairs
- [ ] Start discussing college goals with family
- [ ] Research different types of colleges
- [ ] Create a savings plan for college

---

### 10th Grade (Sophomore Year)

#### Academic Focus
- [ ] Continue rigorous coursework
- [ ] Consider AP or IB courses
- [ ] Prepare for PSAT (practice for SAT)
- [ ] Maintain or improve GPA

#### Testing
- [ ] Take PSAT in October (good practice)
- [ ] Review PSAT results and identify areas for improvement
- [ ] Consider SAT/ACT prep if needed

#### Exploration
- [ ] Continue extracurricular involvement
- [ ] Seek leadership roles in activities
- [ ] Explore career interests through job shadowing
- [ ] Research summer programs at colleges
- [ ] Start informal college visits during family trips

#### Financial Planning
- [ ] Research college costs
- [ ] Discuss budget with family
- [ ] Begin scholarship search
- [ ] Open savings account for college

---

### 11th Grade (Junior Year) - CRITICAL YEAR

#### Academic Focus (Very Important for Admissions)
- [ ] Take most challenging courses available
- [ ] Focus on strong performance (GPA crucial)
- [ ] Meet regularly with counselor

#### Testing (Major Focus)
- [ ] Take PSAT in October (for National Merit consideration)
- [ ] Register for SAT/ACT (spring semester)
- [ ] Take SAT/ACT in spring (March-June)
- [ ] Consider retaking if needed
- [ ] Take SAT Subject Tests if required by colleges

#### College Search
- [ ] Create list of 10-15 colleges of interest
- [ ] Visit college campuses
  - [ ] Schedule official tours
  - [ ] Attend information sessions
  - [ ] Meet with admissions officers
  - [ ] Tour on regular day (see campus in action)
- [ ] Attend college fairs
- [ ] Research college requirements
- [ ] Categorize colleges: Reach, Target, Safety

#### Financial Planning
- [ ] Estimate Expected Family Contribution (EFC)
- [ ] Use net price calculators on college websites
- [ ] Search for scholarships
- [ ] Understand different types of financial aid

#### Summer Before Senior Year
- [ ] Visit more colleges
- [ ] Start drafting college essays
- [ ] Update resume of activities
- [ ] Prepare for early applications
- [ ] Request teacher recommendations

---

### 12th Grade (Senior Year)

#### Fall Semester (Application Season)

**September**
- [ ] Meet with counselor to finalize college list
- [ ] Request official transcripts
- [ ] Ask teachers for recommendation letters
- [ ] Register for Common Application account
- [ ] Start working on essays

**October**
- [ ] Complete FAFSA opening day (October 1)
- [ ] Complete CSS Profile if required
- [ ] Finalize Early Decision/Early Action applications
- [ ] Request test scores sent to colleges
- [ ] Submit Early Decision/Early Action apps (due Nov 1)

**November**
- [ ] Continue working on Regular Decision applications
- [ ] Apply for local scholarships
- [ ] Follow up on recommendation letters
- [ ] Ensure all materials submitted

**December**
- [ ] Submit Regular Decision applications (due Dec 15 - Jan 15)
- [ ] Receive Early Decision/Early Action decisions
- [ ] If accepted ED, withdraw other applications
- [ ] Continue scholarship applications

#### Spring Semester (Decision Season)

**January-March**
- [ ] File taxes early (needed for financial aid verification)
- [ ] Complete any requested financial aid documents
- [ ] Apply for more scholarships
- [ ] Visit colleges again if needed

**April**
- [ ] Receive Regular Decision results
- [ ] Compare financial aid packages
- [ ] Attend accepted student days
- [ ] Make final college decision
- [ ] Submit enrollment deposit by May 1
- [ ] Decline other offers

**May-June**
- [ ] Submit final transcript to chosen college
- [ ] Complete housing application
- [ ] Register for orientation
- [ ] Apply for student loans if needed
- [ ] Accept financial aid package
- [ ] Send thank you notes to recommenders

## College Search Process

### Types of Colleges

#### By Size
- **Small (< 2,000 students)**: Personal attention, small classes
- **Medium (2,000-10,000)**: Balance of resources and community
- **Large (> 10,000)**: Extensive programs, diverse opportunities

#### By Type
- **Public Universities**: State-funded, often larger, less expensive for in-state
- **Private Universities**: Independent funding, often smaller, higher sticker price
- **Liberal Arts Colleges**: Focus on undergraduate education, broad curriculum
- **Community Colleges**: 2-year associate degrees, transfer pathways

#### By Selectivity
- **Highly Selective**: <20% acceptance rate
- **Selective**: 20-50% acceptance rate
- **Moderately Selective**: 50-70% acceptance rate
- **Open Enrollment**: Accept all applicants

---

### Creating Your College List

#### Reach Schools (2-3 colleges)
- Acceptance rate < 25% OR your stats below their average
- "Dream schools" where admission is uncertain
- Still worth applying if you're interested

#### Target Schools (3-5 colleges)
- Your stats match their average admitted student
- Realistic chance of acceptance
- Schools you'd be happy to attend

#### Safety Schools (2-3 colleges)
- Your stats exceed their average
- High likelihood of acceptance
- IMPORTANT: Choose safeties you'd actually attend

**Total List**: 7-11 colleges is typical

---

### College Research Factors

#### Academic Fit
- [ ] Desired major offered and strong?
- [ ] Academic rigor matches ability?
- [ ] Class sizes and student-faculty ratio
- [ ] Research opportunities (for STEM)
- [ ] Study abroad programs
- [ ] Internship/co-op opportunities

#### Campus Environment
- [ ] Location (urban, suburban, rural)
- [ ] Distance from home
- [ ] Campus size and feel
- [ ] Housing options
- [ ] Campus safety
- [ ] Climate and weather

#### Student Life
- [ ] Extracurricular activities
- [ ] Greek life presence
- [ ] Athletics (D1, D2, D3, intramural)
- [ ] Diversity and inclusion
- [ ] Social scene and culture

#### Financial Considerations
- [ ] Total cost of attendance
- [ ] Average financial aid package
- [ ] Merit scholarship availability
- [ ] Work-study opportunities
- [ ] Loan burden and outcomes
- [ ] Return on investment (ROI)

## College Applications

### Application Types

#### Common Application
- 900+ colleges accept it
- One application, multiple schools
- Supplemental essays often required
- commonapp.org

#### Coalition Application
- 150+ colleges
- Alternative to Common App
- coalitionforcollegeaccess.org

#### Individual College Applications
- Some colleges use their own system
- UC system, UT Austin, etc.

---

### Application Components

#### 1. Personal Information
- Basic demographics
- Family information
- School information

#### 2. Academic Record
- Courses and grades
- GPA and class rank
- Test scores (SAT/ACT)
- Transcript submitted by counselor

#### 3. Extracurricular Activities
- List up to 10 activities
- Prioritize by importance/impact
- Include:
  - Activity name
  - Role/position
  - Hours per week and weeks per year
  - Grade levels involved
  - Brief description and achievements

#### 4. Essays

**Common App Personal Statement (650 words)**
- Choose from 7 prompts
- Tell your story
- Show who you are beyond grades
- Be authentic and specific

**Supplemental Essays**
- Specific to each college
- "Why us?" essays
- "Why this major?"
- Additional personal statements
- Word limits vary (200-500 words typical)

#### 5. Letters of Recommendation
- **Counselor Recommendation**: Required for most colleges
- **Teacher Recommendations**: Usually 1-2 required
  - Choose teachers from core subjects (11th or 12th grade)
  - Teachers who know you well
  - Ask early (September of senior year)
  - Provide resume and any helpful information
  - Waive right to view (demonstrates trust)

#### 6. Interview (Optional at most schools)
- Not required for most colleges
- Opportunity to show interest
- Ask questions about the college
- Be prepared to discuss yourself

---

## Essay Writing Tips

### Personal Statement

#### DO:
- Start early and revise multiple times
- Be specific with examples and stories
- Show, don't tell
- Be authentic and genuine
- Focus on growth and reflection
- Proofread carefully

#### DON'T:
- Write what you think they want to hear
- Use clichés or generic statements
- Try to cover too many topics
- Focus only on achievements (show personality)
- Exceed word limit
- Have someone else write it

### Common Essay Topics
1. Overcoming a challenge
2. A meaningful experience or accomplishment
3. An idea or topic you find engaging
4. A person who influenced you
5. Your background and identity
6. Growth and learning from failure

## Financial Aid & Scholarships

### Types of Financial Aid

#### 1. Grants (Free Money - No Repayment)
- **Federal Pell Grant**: Need-based, up to $7,395/year
- **Federal SEOG**: Campus-based need grant
- **State Grants**: Vary by state
- **Institutional Grants**: From the college

#### 2. Scholarships (Free Money - No Repayment)
- **Merit-Based**: Academic achievement, test scores
- **Need-Based**: Financial need
- **Athletic**: Sports talent
- **Talent-Based**: Arts, music, etc.
- **Demographic**: Ethnicity, gender, location
- **Major-Specific**: For certain fields of study
- **Employer**: Parent's employer benefits

#### 3. Work-Study
- Part-time jobs on campus
- Earn money for education expenses
- Need-based program

#### 4. Student Loans (Must Be Repaid)
- **Federal Direct Subsidized**: Need-based, no interest while in school
- **Federal Direct Unsubsidized**: Not need-based, interest accrues
- **Parent PLUS Loans**: For parents, credit check required
- **Private Loans**: From banks, variable rates

---

### FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid)

#### When to File
- Opens October 1 each year
- File as soon as possible (priority deadlines)
- Required for federal aid, state aid, and most institutional aid

#### Information Needed
- Social Security numbers
- Driver's license (if any)
- Federal tax returns (parents and student)
- W-2 forms
- Records of untaxed income
- Bank statements
- Investment records

#### FSA ID
- Create at studentaid.gov
- Serves as electronic signature
- Both student and one parent need FSA ID
- Keep username and password secure

#### Tips
- Use IRS Data Retrieval Tool (auto-imports tax info)
- List colleges in alphabetical order (no priority shown)
- Save FAFSA ID for future years
- Check status online
- Respond to verification requests promptly

---

### CSS Profile

#### What is it?
- College Scholarship Service Profile
- Used by 400+ colleges for institutional aid
- More detailed than FAFSA
- Costs $25 first college, $16 each additional

#### When Required
- Check each college's financial aid requirements
- Often required for private colleges
- Has earlier deadline than FAFSA sometimes

---

### Scholarship Search

#### Where to Find Scholarships

**Free Scholarship Search Sites**
- Fastweb.com
- Scholarships.com
- Cappex.com
- CollegeBoard Scholarship Search
- Niche.com

**Local Scholarships**
- High school counseling office
- Community organizations
- Local businesses
- Parent's employer
- Religious organizations
- Civic groups (Rotary, Lions, etc.)

**College-Specific**
- Check each college's scholarship page
- Merit scholarships (automatic consideration)
- Competitive scholarships (separate application)

#### Scholarship Application Tips
- Start early (junior year)
- Apply to many (numbers game)
- Follow directions exactly
- Meet all deadlines
- Tailor essays to each scholarship
- Keep spreadsheet to track applications
- Reuse and adapt essays
- **NEVER pay to apply** (legitimate scholarships are free)

## Comparing College Offers

### Financial Aid Award Letter

#### Understanding the Letter
```
Cost of Attendance (COA)
- Tuition and fees
- Room and board
- Books and supplies
- Personal expenses
- Transportation

Minus: Financial Aid
- Grants and scholarships (free money)
- Work-study (earned money)
- Loans (borrowed money)

= Net Price (What You Actually Pay)
```

#### Compare Net Price, Not Sticker Price
- College A: $60,000 COA - $40,000 aid = $20,000 net
- College B: $30,000 COA - $5,000 aid = $25,000 net
- **College A is actually cheaper!**

### Making the Final Decision

#### Factors to Consider
1. **Fit**: Do you feel comfortable there?
2. **Academics**: Strong in your major?
3. **Cost**: Can you afford it without excessive debt?
4. **Outcomes**: Graduation rate, job placement
5. **Opportunities**: Internships, research, study abroad

#### General Debt Guidelines
- **Reasonable debt**: Total loans ≤ expected first-year salary
- **Example**: $50,000 starting salary → max $50,000 in loans
- **Federal loan limits**: $27,000 total for dependent undergrads

#### Decision Timeline
- Receive all decisions by early April
- Compare offers
- Revisit top choices if possible
- Submit enrollment deposit by **May 1**
- Decline other offers respectfully
